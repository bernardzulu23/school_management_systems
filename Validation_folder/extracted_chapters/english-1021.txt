Chapter 1: English Language – 1021  
 
1 
Construct 
Demonstrates proficient and competent use of the four language skills (speaking, listening, reading and 
writing) in real life situations adapting to audience and purpose in order to communicate effectively and 
support life-long learning.  
2 
Elements of Construct  
The English Language examination will be based on the following four (4) Elements of Construct (EoC): 
EoC 1   Interprets and understands spoken information 
 Identifies main ideas and supporting details 
 Analyses speaker‘s purpose, tone and intent 
 Follows instructions, directions and questions and responds appropriately 
EoC 2 
Produces ideas, thoughts and opinions through spoken language 
 Organises thoughts and ideas logically 
 Speaks clearly and audibly 
 Uses appropriate vocabulary, expressions and tone for various purposes and actions 
EoC 3 
Reads, Interprets and summarises continuous and non-continuous texts         
 Reads and understands various types of texts 
 Understands and uses words accurately and effectively, considering content, audience and 
purpose 
 Summarises main ideas and key points from a text 
EoC 4  Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions 
 Spells words correctly with orthographic accuracy 
 Applies grammatical rules in sentence construction 
 Punctuates different types of sentence to convey meaning and clarity 
 Produces coherent, purposeful, and grammatically accurate written texts. 
3 
Test Design 
The English Language Examination for Certificate of Secondary Education Ordinary Level (ECSEOL) 
will consist of two components. School Based Assessment (SBA) will be Component 1 and the Final 
Examination will be Component 2. Component 1 will be set and scored at school level in Form 2 and 3 
while Component 2 will be centrally set and marked by the Examinations Council of Zambia at the end of 
Form 4. The weighting for the School Based Assessment and Final Examination will be 30:70 
respectively.

3.1. 
Structure 
3.1.1. 
School Based Assessment 
School Based Assessment will be the based on all the four Elements of Construct which 
focus on the four language skills. The structure of the SBA tasks will be determined by the 
subject teacher with guidance from the details outlined in the School Based assessment 
Guidelines for the Examination for Certificate of Secondary Education Ordinary Level 
(2026) produced by the Examinations Council of Zambia. 
Types of Tasks 
All SBA tasks will be based on all the 4 Elements of Construct which incorporate the four 
language skills. It will consist of the actual use of language in role plays, debates, what 
would you say, group discussions, class tests and individual and group assignments 
 
3.1.2. 
Final Examination 
The final examination will consist of three sections; A, B and C. These sections will be 
based on three out of the four Elements of Construct namely EoC 3: Reads, interprets and 
summarises continuous and non-continuous texts and EoC 4: Demonstrates masterly of 
written language conventions. EoC 4 will be assessed in two parts under Section A and C. 
Types of Items 
The paper will consist of three (3) sections; A, B and C. 
Section A: Structure - 20 marks 
EoC 4: Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions (Structure - 20 marks) 
Scenarios will be used to test the application of grammatical rules and concepts in context 
to convey meaning and purpose.   
Section B: Comprehension and Summary -50 marks 
EoC 3: Reads, Interprets and summarises continuous and non-continuous texts  
Item 1: Comprehension - 30 marks 
Item 1 will contain a scenario presented in a passage and 10 tasks. The tasks will comprise 
of open questions, sequencing and ordering, summarising tasks, application questions, true 
or false with justification, error sporting etc. 
Item 2: Summary - 20 marks  
A passage will be selected to test Summary. This passage will act as a scenario and it will 
be followed by a question on prose summary. 
Section C: Composition - 30 marks 
EoC 4: Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions Composition - 30 marks 
This section will test any type of composition. Candidates will be given two items to 
choose from. The composition will be either guided or unguided. The composition item 
may be based on the comprehension passage or any other independent scenario. 
3.2  
Test Content 
Component 1 - School Based Assessment (SBA) 
SBA will be conducted at Forms 2 and 3 comprising a variety of assessment tasks. Each year, the 
SBA will be conducted out of 100 and the scores submitted to the ECZ. For more details refer to 
School Based Assessment Guidelines for Examination for Certificate of Secondary Education 
Ordinary Level.

Component 2 - Final Examination (FE) 
The Final Examination will be conducted at the end of Form 4. It will consist of one paper with 
three sections. The test items in the three sections will be based on the following two Elements of 
Construct: 
(i)  
(EoC 3): Reads, Interprets and summarises continuous and non-continuous text; and 
(ii) 
(EoC 4): Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions 
Section A will focus on EoC 4, Section B on EoC 3and Section C on EoC 4.  
 
Section A: Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions (Structure - 20 marks) 
Scenarios will be used to come up with items that will test language structures. A variety of 
structures will be tested from year to year. 
 
Section B: Reads, interprets and summarises continuous and non-continuous text (Reading 
Comprehension and Summary – 50 marks) 
Item 1:  Reading Comprehension – 30 marks 
Candidates will be expected to read a given text with understanding and answer follow-up tasks. 
There will be a variety of tasks such as open ended or semi-structured test items. 
Item 2:  Summary - 20 marks  
In this item, candidates will be expected to write a summary based on a selected passage. The 
answer provided will be expected to be in prose or table form.  The prose summary should be 
written within the word limit specified. Information presented in tables should be clear and easy to 
follow. 
The length of the passages for comprehension and summary will range from 450 to 700 words. 
Section C: Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions (Composition-30 marks) 
This section will test any type of composition. Candidates will be given two items to choose from. 
Candidates will be expected to write a continuous piece of writing in accordance with the

3.3 
Level of Difficulty 
The level of difficulty of the questions in the Final English Language assessment will range from 
Remembering to Creating of the cognitive domain of the revised Bloom‘s Taxonomy.  
The difficulty of the items will be determined by the following factors: 
(i) Components of the Scenario: The real-life situation on given Element of Construct that a 
candidate will be expected to respond to correctly. 
(ii) Level of Competence: The subject knowledge and skills required to respond to real-life and 
challenges correctly. 
(iii) The Cognitive Level: The complexity and/or number of cognitive processes required to 
generate responses to items and tasks. 
(iv) The level of abstract and critical thinking involved to generate responses 
(v)   The extent to which a candidate will have to strategise in order to respond to the items or tasks.

4.      Scoring Criteria 
4.1 School Based Assessment – Component 1 
(i) SBA will be scored locally at school by the subject teacher.  
(ii) The teacher should use the Scoring Rubric and Guides stipulated in the School Based Assessment 
Guidelines for the Examination for Certificate of Secondary Education Ordinary level – Literature and 
Languages. 
(iii) All learners scores should be recorded by the subject teacher in the score sheet 9See sample in the 
SBA Guidelines. 
Head Teachers must ensure that once scores have been captured online, a hardcopy is printed and 
sent to the Provincial Education Officers (PEO) through the District Education Board Secretary 
(DEBS) for onwards submission to ECZ. 
 
4.2 Final Examination – Component 2 
Before marking commences, a co-ordination meeting will be held to harmonise the scoring criteria 
for that particular year. Furthermore, during marking, continuous checking of scoring will be done 
to ensure that examiners maintain the standard of scoring by conforming to the scoring criteria.  
The final examination will be marked centrally by the ECZ trained Examiners (Scorers) 
Section A – Demonstrates masterly of written language conventions - Language Structure (20 marks) 
Assessment in structure will focus on learner‘s ability to apply grammatical rules and concepts in context; 
construct clear, effective sentences; demonstrate accuracy and precision in writing, and use language 
structures to convey meaning and purpose. Marks will be allocated to each item. 
Section B - Reads, Interprets and summarises continuous and non-continuous texts - Reading, 
Comprehension and Summary (50 marks)

Candidates will be expected to take part in SBA as well as sit the final examination in order to be

British English should be used in answering the examination questions. American English is

Read the following passage and do the tasks that follow: 
GENDER BASED VIOLENCE 
1. 
Gender-based violence is one of the most pervasive human rights violations across the globe, disproportionately 
affecting women and girls and undermining social, economic, and political progress. It encompasses a wide range 
of harmful acts, including physical, sexual, psychological, and economic abuse, and its impact ripple across 
generations. The United Nations defines gender-based violence as violence that results in, or is likely to result in, 
physical, sexual, or psychological harm or suffering, including threats, coercion, or deprivation of liberty, whether 
occurring in public or private life. While men and boys can also be victims, women and girls are disproportionately 
affected due to entrenched gender inequalities and patriarchal norms that normalise male dominance and female 
subordination. 
2.  
Globally, one in three women experiences physical or sexual violence in her lifetime, most often at the hands of an 
intimate partner. In Zambia, the situation is particularly severe: nearly half of women aged fifteen to forty-nine 
have experienced physical violence since the age of fifteen and almost one in five has faced sexual violence. Early 
marriage compounds the problem, with close to forty percent of Zambian women married before the age of

eighteen, exposing them to lifelong risks of abuse and exploitation. These statistics highlight that gender-based 
violence is not isolated but systemic, rooted in cultural practices, economic inequalities and weak enforcement of 
protective laws. It is a phenomenon that thrives in silence and secrecy, often hidden within households and 
communities, yet its consequences are visible in the health, economic and social fabric of societies. 
3.  
Several factors drive the persistence of gender-based violence. Patriarchal norms remain deeply embedded in many 
cultures, reinforcing unequal power dynamics and legitimising control over women‘s bodies and choices. 
Economic dependence is another critical factor as women who lack financial autonomy are more vulnerable to 
exploitation and abuse. Weak legal frameworks or poor enforcement of existing laws allow perpetrators to act with 
impunity while cultural practices such as child marriage or the payment of bride price reinforce the idea that 
women are possessions rather than individuals with rights. Conflict and displacement further exacerbate the 
problem as instability increases the risks of sexual violence, exploitation and trafficking. 
4.  
The consequences of gender-based violence are profound and multidimensional. Survivors often suffer physical 
injuries, unwanted pregnancies, sexually transmitted infection, and long-term psychological trauma. The economic 
impacts are equally devastating, reducing productivity, increasing healthcare costs, and perpetuating cycles of 
poverty. In Zambia, thousands of criminal cases and even murders have been linked to gender-based violence, 
straining social services and the justice system. Socially, violence erodes trust within families and communities, 
perpetuating cycles of inequality and marginalisation. Children exposed to violence are more likely to experience 
trauma, poor educational outcomes and to perpetuate violence in adulthood, creating a generational cycle that is 
difficult to break. 
5.  
Efforts to combat gender-based violence must be comprehensive and multi-sectoral. Strong laws against domestic 
violence, sexual assault, and child marriage must not only be enacted but also enforced, with survivors given 
access to justice systems that protect them from retaliation. Education and awareness campaigns are vital in 
challenging harmful norms and promoting gender equality, while schools should integrate gender sensitivity and 
rights education into their curricula. Economic empowerment is another crucial strategy as providing women with 
access to education, employment, and financial independence reduces vulnerability and strengthens resilience. 
Support services such as shelters, counselling, and healthcare are essential for survivors while hotlines and safe 
reporting mechanisms encourage victims to seek help without fear. Importantly, engaging men and boys in the 
fight against gender-based violence is critical. Transforming attitudes among men through programmes that 
promote positive masculinity can reduce violence and foster healthier relationships. 
 
6.  
Gender-based violence is not only a violation of individual rights but also a barrier to national development and 
social justice. It perpetuates cycles of poverty, inequality and trauma, undermining the well-being of entire 
communities. Addressing it requires legal, social and cultural transformation, alongside strong political will and 
grassroots activism. Ultimately, the fight against gender-based violence is a fight for human dignity, equality and 
freedom. Every society that aspires to progress must confront this issue head-on, ensuring that women and girls 
live free from fear and violence. Only then can communities unlock their full potential and build futures rooted in 
justice and equality.

Chapter 2: Literature in English – 1025  
1. 
Construct 
Demonstrates literary skills and creativity, communicates effectively through competent use of language 
in real life situations and appreciates diverse cultures. 
2. 
Elements of Construct 
The Literature in English examination will be assessed based on the following four (4) Elements of 
Construct: 
EoC1 
Acquires literary skills and uses them in everyday life  
 Acquires both the theoretical knowledge and practical literary skills 
 Analyses and produces literary works with confidence and creativity 
 Uses literary skills in day to day interactions for communication and entrepreneurial 
purposes. 
EoC2 
Analyses and applies the elements of the literary genre of poetry 
 Analyses the elements of poetry in a prescribed text  
 Appreciates the role that poetry plays in society 
 Explores critical thinking, analytical abilities and self -awareness 
EoC3 
Analyses and applies the elements of the literary genre of drama   
 Analyses the elements of drama in a prescribed text  
 Appreciates the role that drama plays in everyday life

EoC4 
Analyses and applies elements of the literary genre of prose  
 Analyses the elements of prose in a prescribed text 
 Appreciates the role that prose plays in the nation 
 Encourages active participation in creation of various literary works 
3.

will consist of two components. Component 1 will be School Based Assessment (SBA) -Component 1

centrally set by Examination Council of Zambia. The SBA-Component 1 will be conducted at Form 2 and

one theory paper administered at the end of Form 4. The SBA (Component 1) will carry 30 percent of the 
total examination score while the Final Examination (Component 2) will carry 70 percent.

3.1 
Test Structure 
School Based Assessment - Component 1 will have eight (8) SBA tasks for Form 2 and seven (7) 
for Form 3. The first six tasks will be done in the first two terms of each form. The third term of 
Form 2 will have two (2) tasks while the third term of Form 3 will have one (1) tasks. Candidates 
will be expected to fulfil all the requirements of the assigned tasks. 
In Component 2, there will be three sections.  Section A will have one item on literary terminologies 
and devices and/or the history of Zambian literature. Section B will have four extracts from the four 
studied/prescribed texts on poetry and drama while Section C will comprise ten essay items based 
on the five prescribed texts on prose.  
3.2  
Types of Tasks and Items 
School Based Assessments - Component 1  
All School-Based Assessment tasks should be semi-structured and subjective types of tasks 
that will engage the learners in various activities. 
Final Examination - Component 2 
The item types in Section A will consist of objective (short responses) and subjective items, 
the ones in Section B will consist of contextual items while Section C will consist of 
subjective items. 
3.2 
Test Content 
The SBA at Forms 2 and 3 will assess all competences outlined in the syllabus at either form level.  
The Final Examination Paper will include all topics from the syllabus from Forms 1 to 4. 
Section A – Literary Terminologies and Devices 
This section will focus on EoC 1: Acquires literary skills and uses them in everyday life. 
The section will assess candidates‘ understanding and application of literary terminologies and 
devices, and the history of Zambian Literature. The total marks will be 20. 
Section B – Poetry and Drama 
This section will focus on two Elements of Construct (contextual) namely: 
EoC 2:  Analyses and applies the elements of the literary genre of poetry; and  
EoC 3: Analyses and applies the elements of the literary genre of drama. 
This section will have 4 items based on four prescribed texts: two (2) on poetry and two (2) on 
drama. There will be four (4) extracts from the prescribed set texts followed by interrogative and 
analytical tasks and candidates will be required to provide concise and accurate responses with close 
reference to the given extract and the studied text as a whole. Candidates will have to attempt an 
item on poetry and another on drama. Two of the prescribed texts (one on poetry and one on drama) 
should be authored by Zambians.  The total marks will be 40. 
Section C - Prose 
This section will focus on EoC 4: Analyses and applies the elements of the literary genre of 
prose (Essay Writing) 
The section will assess candidates through essays. Candidates will be assessed on two (2) studied 
set texts chosen from a list of five prescribed texts. Three of these texts should be authored by 
Zambian writers. Candidates will be expected to answer two (2) items on two (2) different texts. 
One of the texts should be written by a Zambian. Each essay will carry 20 marks. Total marks for 
this section will be 40.

EoC 4

 Integrates cultural understanding and personal responsibility to promote   integrity, combat

3.1 
Test Structure 
School Based Assessment – Component 1 
 The subject will use all types of questions such as structured, semi-structured and open ended. 
These will be in form of class tests, debates, projects, group discussions, presentations, role play 
and assignments. The project will start in Form 2 and finalised in Form 3. For GCE candidates, an 
alternative paper to SBA will be developed by the teacher. Guidance will be provided by the ECZ. 
Final Examination - Component 2 
The Final Examination theory paper will consist of two sections: Section A and Section B. 
Component 2 will be based on the four elements of construct, comprising a total of six (6) items. 
Section A will cover Elements 1 and 2, each with two items. Candidates will be required to answer
