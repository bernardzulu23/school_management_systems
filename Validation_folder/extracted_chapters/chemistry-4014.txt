Chapter 15: Chemistry – 4014  
1. 
Construct 
Applies chemistry concepts and scientific skills in solving real life problems. 
2. 
Elements of Construct 
The Chemistry examination will be based on the following six (6) Elements of Construct (EOC): 
EoC1 
Classifies substances and interprets the physical and chemical changes of the substances 
for suitable application in life 
• 
Classifies substances according to their nature, properties and their application in real life.  
• 
Investigates and Interprets the physical and chemical changes of substances 
EoC2 
Analyses and identifies chemical composition of substances for scientific decision making.  
• 
Uses a variety of analytical procedures, methods and techniques involved in identifying 
the chemical composition of substances in solid, liquid or gaseous form in order to solve 
real life problems. 
EoC3 
Evaluates industrial chemical processes and suggests alternative methods of production  
that are sustainable and environmentally safe 
• 
Investigates and Evaluates the conditions employed in industrial chemical processes and 
how these processes impact on the environment.  
• 
Generates alternative methods of production that are sustainable and environmentally 
friendly.  
EoC4: 
Applies Chemistry principles and mathematical operations to determine quantities of 
either reactants or products from quantitative (balanced) chemical equations in real-life 
situations or for solving problems 
• 
Applies mathematical manipulations to interpret stoichiometric ratios from balanced   
chemical equations. 
• 
Determines stoichiometric quantities of reactants and their products from balanced 
chemical equations.   
EoC5 
Investigates quantities involving industrial processes 
• 
Determines theoretical yields in various industrial chemical processes as applied by 
chemists in everyday life. 
• 
Develops more efficient ways of increasing yields. 
EoC6 
Applies Chemistry principles in everyday life 
• 
Designs and develops solutions, products and services using acquired scientific knowledge 
and skills for local and global real-life situations.   
3. 
Test Design 
3.1  
Structure  
The Chemistry examination will be made up of two components namely Component 1 and 2. The 
School Based Assessment will be component 1 while the final examination will be component 2. 
3.1.1 School Based Assessment: Component 1 
The assessments in SBA will be administered during the course of teaching and learning at 
school level from Form 2 Term 1 to Form 3 Term 3. It shall account for 30% of the total 
assessment. The structure of SBA tasks will be determined by the type of task and by the 
teacher.  
3.1.2 Final Examination: Component 2

3.2 
Test Content  
 
The test content for both components 1 and 2 will be considered in terms of test items, level 
of difficulty and syllabus coverage. 
3.2.1 
School Based Assessment: Component 1 
The SBA tasks will be based on all the six Elements of Construct. These tasks will be   
done during the teaching and learning process in the school as formative and summative 
assessments. 
Types of Tasks 
Component 1 will comprise tests, assignments, practical and research/project work among 
other tasks.  All the tasks will consist of a scenario depicting a real-life situation containing 
a problem or challenge. The items will include semi-structured items 
3.2.2 
Final Examination Component 2 
This will consist of semi-structured and open ended type of items. There shall be six (6) 
compulsory items based on scenarios depicting real life problems. 
Semi-Structured item: These items shall include a scenario presented as a real-life 
situation, in form of a diagram, graph, table, picture, or a short stem, followed by a number 
of sub-tasks. Candidates shall be required to provide answers using words, numbers, 
symbols, phrases, definitions, comparisons and calculations. 
Open ended: These tasks and items will require candidates to answer in complete 
sentences and paragraphs, presenting their responses in a logical and coherent manner in 
order to respond to the competence being sought. 
3.2.3 
Level of Difficulty 
The level of difficulty of tasks/items in the Chemistry assessments for School Based 
Assessment will range from remembering to creating level of the Revised Bloom‘s 
Taxonomy while the level of difficulty for the Final Examination items will range from 
remembering to Evaluating level. The level of difficulty will be determined by the 
following factors:

3.2.4 
Syllabus Coverage 
The Chemistry assessments will be based on the competences in the Form 1–4 Physics 
syllabus (CDC 2024). Both assessment components will ensure full coverage of the 
Elements of Construct by incorporating a cross-representation of the key competences in 
Physics. 
In Component 2, a Test framework has been developed to show the number of items that 
will be required to be set for each Element of Construct.  
Some items may introduce information that is unfamiliar to the candidates. In responding 
to such tasks, learners are required to use the competences developed through the syllabus 
such as reasoning, analysing, interpreting evidence, and applying Chemistry concepts to 
solve problems in new or unfamiliar situations. This aligns fully with the competency-
based approach, which emphasises on the use of acquired competences in solving practical, 
real-life challenges. 
4. 
Scoring Criteria 
The following scoring criteria will govern the evaluation of the School Certificate and General Certificate 
of Education Chemistry assessments for both School Based Assessment (SBA) - Component 1 and Final 
Examination - Component 2 
4.1 
School-Based Assessment (SBA) - Component 1 
Teachers will develop and use appropriate scoring tools such as scoring guides and scoring rubrics 
for each task e.g. end of year tests. These scoring tools will ensure consistency, fairness, and 
accuracy when evaluating learners‘ performance in activities such as end of term or year tests, 
practical work, assignments, and other semi-structured items. The scoring criteria will reflect the 
expected Chemistry competences as outlined in the syllabus. 
The scoring criteria will measure not only the correctness of responses but also the application of 
scientific skills such as observation, data interpretation, problem-solving, communication, and use 
of scientific procedures. This approach ensures accuracy, consistency, and full alignment with the 
competence-based learning activities in the Form 1-4 Chemistry syllabus (CDC 2024). 
4.2  
Final Examination - Component 2 
The final examination will consist of specific scoring procedures to maintain acceptable standards. 
The scoring criteria will be provided by the Examinations Council of Zambia.

5. 
Notes and Guidelines 
This section highlights important notes and subject-specific guidelines that candidates and teachers must 
follow during the administration and scoring of the chemistry assessments. These guidelines ensure 
fairness, consistency, and alignment with the competence-based chemistry curriculum. 
5.1 
General Examination Guidelines 
5.1.1 
Candidates must sit for both components to be graded. 
5.1.2 
All responses for component 2 must be written in the spaces provided within the question 
paper. 
5.1.3 
Cell phones and other unauthorized devices and materials are not allowed in the 
examination room. 
5.2  
Chemistry-Specific Guidelines 
5.2.1 
Diagrams must be large, neat, correctly labelled, and drawn with a sharp pencil. Shading 
and colouring are not allowed unless instructed. Labelling of diagrams should be done 
using names and not chemical formulae, and arrows should not be used as label lines, 
except when intended to show direction of movement. 
5.2.2 
Chemical symbols in chemical formulae must be written as defined on the Periodic Table 
5.2.3 
On a chemical equation, state symbols should be written in brackets in the same line as 
symbols/formulae and not as subscripts. 
5.2.4 
A chemical equation should be written in the same line, and an arrow should separate the 
reactants and the products. Two half-headed arrows shall be used for reversible 
reactions/equilibrium. 
5.2.5 
When answering data-based chemistry items, candidates must show working steps, units, 
and explanations where required. 
5.2.6 
For tasks involving experiments or investigations, candidates must follow the scientific 
method, showing observations, reasoning, and conclusions. 
5.2.7 
Scientific terminologies must be used accurately. Vague non-scientific statements will not 
earn full marks. 
5.2.8 
In items involving unfamiliar scenarios or materials, candidates must apply principles and 
concepts from the syllabus to make logical conclusions.  
5.2.9      In research projects, learners must design realistic products that are workable, use locally 
available or improvised materials and solve local or community problems. 
6.  
Exemplar Test Items 
Exemplar items are sample test items provided to illustrate the type, format, and level of difficulty of 
questions that may appear in an assessment. They serve as a guide for both teachers and learners by 
showing how knowledge, skills, and competences can be assessed in practice. Exemplar items demonstrate 
the alignment of assessment test items with specific competences and the desired cognitive levels, helping 
to ensure that teaching and evaluation are consistent with the curriculum. In a competency-based 
assessment, exemplar items also show how learners are expected to apply knowledge and skills in 
practical real-life situations, highlighting the specific competences targeted by each task.

Example 6 
EoC6: Applies chemistry principles in every day life. 
Item 
You live in a family house not connected to the main electricity grid, 
resulting in experiences of energy deficit. During meal preparation, 
your family kitchen generates peels from vegetables and fruits, along 
with other carbohydrate-rich substances, which are normally treated 
as waste and disposed of. 
Now do the following Tasks: 
(i) As a Chemistry learner, describe a design of a system that can be 
used to solve the energy deficit in your household using 
materials from the immediate environment.                                     
[4 marks] 
(ii) Sketch a labelled diagram showing the key features of the design 
you described in (i). [3 marks] 
(iii) Write a balanced chemical equation for the main reaction that 
produces the energy source in your design. (State any 
conditions required.) [2 marks] 
(iv) Calculate the amount of energy (in kJ) produced per hour by 
your design if the system processes 85 g of carbohydrate-rich 
food waste per hour. 
    Total 14 marks 
Relevance:  
       Contextual relevance: The scenario (energy deficit + kitchen 
peels/fruits waste) is authentic for rural/off-grid Zambian 
households. The expected solution is clearly a household 
biogas digester (anaerobic digestion of carbohydrate-rich 
waste→biogas→cooking/light/electricity). This directly applies 
organic 
chemistry 
concepts 
(fermentation, 
anaerobic 
decomposition) to solve an energy crisis. 
       Competence focus: Forces learners to use “immediate 
environment” (kitchen waste) rather than imported fuels, which 
is precisely what is required for CBC. 
 
Answer:  
(i) Award 4 marks for a clear description of a system 
using local materials: 
 
Processing the raw materials. 
 
Input system 
 
Output system 
 
Energy collection 
 
Reaction conditions. (1 mark each for key 
features; max 4) 
(ii) Award up to 3 marks for a neat, labelled sketch 
showing: inlet, main digester tank, gas 
holder/space, outlet for slurry, gas pipe to burner 
(labels essential). 
(iii) Balanced equation (e.g anaerobic digestion of 
glucose):  
C6H12O6  3CH4 + 3CO2 
      (Conditions: e.g anaerobic bacteria, mesophilic 
temperature ~30–40 °C, sealed digester) Award 
2 marks for correct balanced equation + mention 
of anaerobic conditions). 
(iv) Step-by-step calculation (award marks for each 
correct step): 
1. Molar mass of glucose = 180 g/mol [1 Mark] 
Moles of glucose processed per hour = 85 g / 
180 g/mol = 0.472 mol (≈0.4722) [1 mark] 
2. From equation:  
1 mol glucose → 3 mol CH₄ 
 Moles of CH₄ = 3 × 0.4722  
= 1.4166 mol [1 mark] 
3. Biogas is 60% CH₄ → actual CH₄ moles same 
(volume % ≈ mole % at same T,P), but energy 
is from CH₄ only.  
Energy from combustion =  
moles CH₄ × 890 kJ/mol  
= 1.4166 × 890 ≈ 1261 kJ  
(or precisely 1.4167 × 890 
 = 1260.863 ≈ 1261 kJ) [2 marks for steps; 1 mark 
for final answer] Final answer: 1261 kJ per hour 
(accept 1260–1261 kJ depending on rounding) 
The item is at multiple skill levels requiring good application of the Chemistry principles to solve real-life problems. 
Key competences required are analytical and critical thinking, problem solving, communication and environmental 
sustainability.
