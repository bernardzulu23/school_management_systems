Chapter 4: Religious Education – 3012  
1. 
Construct 
Demonstrates understanding of the interconnections between God and spirituality, humanity and the 
environment by interpreting religious teachings and practices and applies them to real life situations 
2. 
Elements of Construct (EoC) 
The Religious Education examination for Certificate of Secondary Education Ordinary Level (ECSEOL) 
will be based on the following five Elements of Construct: 
EoC 1 
Demonstrates understanding of growth, friendship and family as basis for human existence 
• Demonstrates and applies personal development, learning, and self-improvement. 
• Appreciates supportive relationships, trust and shared experiences. 
• Demonstrates family bonds, love and sense of belonging. 
EoC 2 
Demonstrates understanding of spirituality, faith and prayer as commitment to God 
• Appreciates spirituality, faith and prayer as sources of strength, guidance and comfort.  
EoC 3 
Applies national values and principles in the community 
• Comprehends and applies values and principles for the benefit of one self and the community. 
EoC 4 
Demonstrates understanding of man’s attitude to Life and work 
• Demonstrates positive attitudes to life and work. 
EoC 5 
Demonstrates understanding of order, leadership and freedom in society  
• Appreciates and applies order, leadership, integrity and freedom in society. 
3. 
Test Design 
The Religious Education examination will consist of two components; component 1 and 2.  
SBA -Component 1 will be School Based Assessment (SBA) which will be set and scored at school. The 
SBA will be administered in Form 2 and 3. 
 Final Examination-Component 2 will be set and scored by the Examination council of Zambia at the 
end of Form 4. 
SBA will be weighted 30% and Final Examinations will be weighted 70%.

          3.2         Test Content  
The test content for both Component 1 and 2 will be considered in terms of question types, 
level of difficulty and syllabus coverage. 
3.2.1 
 Types of Tasks and Items 
SBA - Component 1: Will consist of subjective tasks which will be structured, semi 
structured, unstructured and essays. The tasks will be in form of written assignment, 
Discussions, quizzes and simple projects from all the specific competences and Elements of 
Construct for Religious Education  
(see details in the School Based assessment Guidelines for Examination for Certificate of 
Secondary Education Ordinary Level for Social Sciences), produced by the ECZ).  
Final Examination - Component 2: Will consist of two items per Element of Construct 
giving a total of 10 items. Each item will carry 20 marks. The candidates will be expected to 
answer, one from each Elements of Construct. The items will consist of semi-structured and 
essays. The ten items will present real life scenarios with a problem or challenge that will 
require a candidate‘s interpretation and problem solving. Candidates are expected to 
demonstrate their communication, collaboration, emotional intelligence, citizenship, critical 
thinking, analytical thinking and problem-solving skills.  
3.2.2 
Syllabus Coverage 
SBA tasks and Final Examination items and will be based on the content from the Religious 
Education Syllabus for Secondary Education Ordinary Level developed by Curriculum 
Development Centre (CDC), 2024.  
The development of School Based Assessment tasks and Final Examination items will be 
aligned to the key competences such as analytical thinking, citizenship, collaboration 
creativity and innovation, critical thinking, communication, digital literacy, problem 
solving, entrepreneurship, financial literacy, environmental sustainability and emotional 
intelligence.

The items and tasks will be linked to related competences which have been grouped into 
Elements of Construct.  
The specific competences are reflected in the SBA Test Framework, Test Framework and 
Test Specification table produced by the ECZ. These assessment tools will ensure validity, 
reliability and comparability of the assessments from year to year.   
3.2.3 
Level of Difficulty 
The level of difficulty for test items in Components 1 and 2 will assess the level of 
competences acquired through knowledge acquisition and application of skills across all the 
six levels of the revised Bloom‘s taxonomy.  
SBA - Component 1: The application of skills will be assessed mainly through applying, 
analysing, evaluating and creating. Real life problems will be presented in different 
scenarios based on Elements of Construct and then tasks given to interpret the scenarios in 
order to solve problem.  
Final Examination - Component 2: The acquisition of knowledge will be assessed 
through understanding of concepts and applying them to real life scenarios from Elements 
of Construct in order to respond to the real life problems.   
Both components will be Competence Based Assessments (CBA) which will be presented 
in real life scenarios. Candidates will need to apply critical thinking, creativity and problem-
solving skills in order to come up with expected responses. 
The levels of difficulty for test items and tasks will be determined by the following factors: 
(i) 
The complexity of the scenario or real-life situation on a given Element of Construct 
that a candidate will be expected to respond to.    
(ii) 
The subject knowledge and skills required to respond to the real-life problems and 
challenges (level of competence). 
(iii) The complexity and number of cognitive processes required to generate responses to 
test items and tasks. 
(iv) 
The level of analytical and critical thinking involved to arrive at a response. 
 
(v) 
The extent to which a candidate will have to strategise in order to respond to the 
items and tasks 
4. 
Scoring Criteria 
Before marking commences, a co-ordination meeting will be held organised by ECZ to harmonise the 
scoring guide or scoring rubric for that particular year. Furthermore, during scoring, continuous checking 
of scoring will be done to ensure that examiners maintain the standard of scoring by conforming to the 
scoring guide. 
5. 
Notes and Guidelines 
5.1 
Teachers and learners will use Religious scriptures for all four main religions. 
5.2 
Teachers should teach in such a way that however the question comes in each section, learners will 
be able to answer it. 
5.3 
Each item should be answered in prose and narrative forms. 
5.4 
Teachers and learners should read relevant scriptures/books and Good News Bible.

6. 
Exemplar Test Items 
 
EoC 1: Demonstrates understanding of growth, friendship and family as base for human existence. 
Item  
In Chidano village in Zambia lived a polygamous man who had two wives and six children. However, he had not yet 
fulfilled marital obligations of bride price payment and Church wedding with any of the two wives. He chased the 
second wife because she committed adultery (marital unfaithfulness). She left behind her three children with her 
husband and her co- wife. Her co-wife wife then took responsibility of looking after all the six children. The co-wife 
now feels that it was time for the step children to leave. The husband was not in agreement with her idea and felt all the 
children should stay in his home. 
 
Tasks (Courtship and Marriage) 
(a) 
Explain the Christian values the scenario teaches young people about marriage.  
 [6 marks] 
Answers 
It teaches young people: 
− 
To marry monogamously. 
− 
To love their marriage partners. 
− 
Should uphold the value of faithfulness in marriage. 
− 
For example, young ladies learn to be submissive to their husbands. 
− 
That love is kind and patient, which are key values in marriage. 
− 
To practice marriage between one husband and one wife just as God created one-woman eve for one-man 
Adam.  
(b) 
How can Traditional marriage approaches be used to promote stability of such a marriage in the Scenario?  [6 
marks] 
Answers 
− 
The man ought to fulfil the obligation of bride price payment so that the remaining woman can appreciate 
her place in this marriage as wife, for the stability of marriage.  
− 
The remaining co-wife should be submissive to the man.   
− 
She ought to fulfil the aspirations/ responsibility of taking care of all the children to promote peace/ 
harmony between the man, woman and children which is important for marital stability. 
− 
The remaining co-wife should respect and love all the children in this family. 
− 
Both parties should involve elders for support and resolve the problem. 
− 
The man should forgive the wife he has chased. 
(c) 
Identify factors that promote effective communication in marriage.  
 [8 marks] 
Answers 
− 
Active listening: paying attention, understanding and responding thoughtfully. 
− 
Empathy: putting yourself in your partner‘s shoes and showing understanding. 
− 
Honesty: being open and truthful even when it‘s tough. 
− 
Respect: speaking kindly and respectfully, even in disagreements. 
− 
Clear expression: clearly expressing thoughts, feelings and needs. 
− 
Timing: choosing the right moment to discuss important issues. 
− 
Feedback: giving and receiving constructive feedback. 
− 
Apologising: being willing to apologise and forgive. 
− 
Openness: being willing to share thoughts, feelings and desires. 
− 
Non- verbal cues: paying attention to body language, tone and facial expressions. 
Level of difficulty: 1) understanding, 2) Evaluating 3) Analysing    
Specific competence: Applying emotional intelligence in marital relationships 
Key competences: communication, critical thinking, analytical thinking, problem solving

EoC 2: Appreciates spirituality, faith and prayer as commitment to God. 
Item   
Mandingo who lives in Chilindi village lost his aunt and uncle within a space of one week. He is wondering why this 
has happened to his family. He is planning to go and consult the diviner to find out the root cause of these mysterious 
deaths in his family. 
Tasks (Death and Life After Death) 
(a) 
You are a nephew to Mandingo; explain to him the various beliefs on death. [6 marks] 
Death is: 
− 
Caused by witch craft. 
− 
The will of God. 
− 
Not the end of life. 
− 
Transition. 
− 
Caused by natural disasters. 
− 
Caused by diseases. 
− 
Caused by accidents. 
(b) 
Identify ways in which people in modern society prepare for death.  
 [8 marks] 
− 
Advance directives: Creating living wills, powers of attorney and do not resuscitate orders to outline 
preferences. 
− 
Estate Planning: Drafting wills, setting up trusts and designating beneficiaries for assets like life insurance 
and retirement accounts. 
− 
Funeral planning: pre- paying for funerals, choosing burial plots or opting for cremation services. 
− 
Legacy planning: creating letters, videos, or journals for loved ones, or establishing charitable foundations. 
− 
Organ donation: registering to donate organs or tissues after death. 
− 
Spiritual preparation: engaging in rituals, prayers, or meditation to cope with mortality. 
− 
Life Insurance: purchasing policies to provide financial support for the loved ones after death. 
− 
Palliative care: focusing on pain management and comfort in terminal illnesses. 
− 
Health and wellness: prioritizing healthy lifestyles to potentially extend life expectancy. 
− 
Digital legacy: managing digital presence, including social media accounts and online assets. 
(c) 
Analyse many different way people react to death.  
[4 marks] 
There are many different ways people react to death: 
− 
Blame it on witch craft 
− 
Blame it on others 
− 
Blame it on God 
− 
Accept it as a will of God 
(d) 
Examine Islamic teachings on death and life after death.  
[ 6 marks] 
− 
Every Muslim believes that death follows life, but it is not the end. There is life after death. 
− 
One‘s deeds will be rewarded or punished depending upon how one has lived in the present life. 
− 
 They may go first to the mosque, where prayers are conducted. Mourners stand in 3 rows and prayers are 
led by an Imam. 
Level of difficulty: (a) understanding, (b) Applying (c) evaluating (d) Evaluating   
Specific competence: Demonstrate understanding of death 
Key competences: communication, critical thinking, analytical thinking, problem solving

EoC 3: Understands and applies values and principles in the Community. 
Item   
In a quiet town, there was a secondary school learner named Mwanja. She noticed a friend Katala was cheating in an 
important examination.  Mwanja knew that reporting her friend could lead to severe consequences, including suspension 
or expulsion from school. However, she felt she would be guilt if he did not report the incident as it would mean 
allowing dishonesty to go unpunished. Mwanja found herself in a moral dilemma. She decided to seek advice from her 
teacher, who encouraged her to think critically about the situation. After reflecting on her values, Mwanja chose to talk 
to Katala first. She explained the importance of being honesty and suggested that Katala confesses to the teacher. 
Together, they approached the teacher, and while Katala faced consequences, she learned a valuable lesson about 
integrity. 
Tasks  
(a) 
Describe the moral dilemma faced by Mwanja according to the scenario.  
 [6 marks] 
(b) 
How did Mwanja apply critical thinking to solve the moral dilemma?   [6 marks] 
(c) 
Analyse the consequences of moral and immoral actions.  
 [8 marks] 
Level of difficulty: a) remembering understanding b) analysing c) Analysing   
Specific competence: Apply National Principles and Values  
Key competences: communication, critical thinking, analytical thinking, problem solving 
EoC 4: Demonstrates understanding of man’s attitude to Life and work. 
Item   
―Do not cheat poor and needy hired servants, whether fellow- Israelites or foreigners living in one of your towns. Each 
day before sunset pay them for that day‘s work, they need the money and have counted on getting it. If you do not pay 
them, they will cry out against you to the Lord and you will be guilty of sin‖.   (Deuteronomy 24:14-15) 
Tasks  
(a) 
What is the main message in the passage?  
 [6 marks] 
− 
Not to cheat the poor 
− 
Not to mistreat the needy or hired servants. 
− 
Pay a labourer before sunset 
− 
 Pay a hired servant for the day‘s work  
(b) 
How can the message in the passage strengthen the ethics at work of the employers in giving good conditions of 
service to their employees in Zambia today?  
 [6 marks] 
− 
Oppression of the poor is bad. 
− 
Exploitation of workers is wrong. 
− 
Withholding of wages from a worker is sin. 
− 
The employer should be fair and not use their position to threaten anyone. 
(c) 
Explain the application of Islamic teachings on ethics at work that foster good conditions of service at the places 
of work.  
[8 marks] 
− 
A master who has a servant under his authority let him feed him out of what he eats himself 
− 
Let him cloth him out of what he clothes himself. 
− 
A master should not charge his servant with work beyond his power. 
− 
If the master entrusts his servant with work beyond his power, let him help him in it. 
− 
Pay the labourer his wages before his sweat gets dry. 
− 
The employer should never act unjustly or unkindly towards his employee.  
Level of difficulty: a) Understanding b) Analyising c) Application  
Specific competence: Analyse teachings of the four religions on work 
Key competences: communication, emotional intelligent, critical thinking, analytical thinking, problem solving
