Chapter 12: Mathematics II – 2025  
1. 
Construct 
Demonstrates spatial reasoning, logical, critical and analytical thinking in solving real life problems. 
 
2.  
Elements of construct 
The Mathematics II examination will be based on the following five (5) Elements of Construct (EoC):  
EoC 1  Interprets and computes relationships of numbers within and across different number 
systems 
 Manipulate numbers using different properties to solve real life problems 
 Creates patterns and puzzles of different types of numbers 
EoC 2 
Models real life situations to solve problems and make informed decisions 
 Use algebra to express and solve real life problems 
EoC 3  Computes measures and applies spatial relationships to solve real life problems 
 Applies measures and geometry in life contexts to solve problems  
 Derives theorems and establishes formulae for different types of measures 
 Demonstrates how to obtain the value of pi () practically. 
EoC 4  Interprets and computes functions to solve real life situations in order to make informed 
decisions  
 Interprets functions, rates of change and summation of quantities in life contexts to solve 
problems  
EoC 5 
Presents and analyses data to make informed decisions  
 Presents and Interprets data in a variety of real-life contexts 
3. 
Test Design 
The Mathematics II examination will consist of two components: Component 1 and 2. Component 1 will 
be School Based Assessment (SBA) which will be set and scored by the teachers at school level. SBA will 
be administered in Form 2 and 3. Component 2 will be the Final Examination which will be centrally set 
and marked by the Examinations Council of Zambia.  The Final Examination will be administered at the 
end of Form 4. The weighting of SBA will be 30% and the weighting for the Final Examination will be 
70%.

The test design for Mathematics II will be considered in terms of structure and test content. 
3.1 
Structure 
School Based Assessment tasks will be conducted in Form 2 and Form 3 and will be part of the 
teaching and learning process. The SBA tasks will be based on all the five Elements of Construct.

The SBA tasks that will contribute towards the learner‘s final score will be administered in Form 2 
and Form 3. There will be a total of twelve (12) tasks that will contribute towards a learner‘s final 
grade; six (6) in Form 2 and six (6) in Form 3 giving a total of 200 marks.  
Final Examination - will comprise two sections, Section A and Section B. Section A will have two 
compulsory items and Section B will have four items and candidates will answer three. The Final 
Examination will comprise scenario-based items from real life that will require candidates to apply 
mathematical concepts and skills. The items in the Final Examination will be based on all the five 
Elements of Construct. 
3.1.1 
School Based Assessments (SBA): Component 1 
School Based Assessment will be conducted in Form 2 and Form 3.  The SBA tasks will be 
based on all the five (5) Elements of construct. The total marks for SBA for each Form 
level will be 100 giving a total 200. School Based Assessments in Form 2 and Form 3 will 
be prepared by teachers and will contribute 30% to the final grade of a candidate.  
3.1.2 
Final Examination: Component 2  
The Final Examination will consist of six (6) items. The items will be based on the all the 
five (5) Elements of Construct. Candidates will answer a total of five items: two from 
Section A and three from Section B. The total number of marks for the Final Examination 
will be 100 and will contribute 70% to the final grade of a candidate. The duration for the 
Final Examination will be 2 hours 30 minutes. 
It is important to base both the SBA tasks and the Final Examination items on all the EoC and key 
competences in order to adequately evaluate the acquisition of the competences by the learners.   
3.2 
Test Content 
 
The test content for both Component 1 and 2 will be considered in terms of Task/Item types, 
syllabus coverage and level of difficulty.  
3.2.1 
Types of Items and Tasks 
 School Based Assessments: Component 1 - will comprise objective and subjective tasks 
from real life situations. The tasks will include assignments, presentations, class exercises, 
practical activities, quizzes and tests by individual learners or in groups. SBA tasks will 
include a project that is subject specific. The SBA tasks should enable learners to acquire 
the specific competences outlined in the syllabus and also develop key competences such as 
collaboration, communication, digital literacy, critical thinking, financial literacy, analytical 
thinking, problem-solving skills and creativity and innovation, that are important for them 
to find solutions to problems in real life.  
 
Final Examination: Component 2 – will consist of two (2) compulsory items in Section A 
which will be scenario based. Each item will have several tasks. Section B will have four 
(4) items which will be scenario based and each item will have several tasks. Candidates 
will answer three items in Section B. Each item will have a total of 20 marks giving a total 
of 100 marks for Component 2. The six scenarios will come from the five EoC and the 
tasks that will follow will be based on the scenarios provided. Candidates will apply the 
key and specific competences in terms of knowledge and skills acquired at this level in 
order to find solutions to the problems.

The tasks and items in Component 1 and 2 respectively will assess competences in all the 
five Elements of Construct derived from the Mathematics II Syllabus: Secondary Education

3.2.3 
Level of difficulty 
The level of difficulty for School Based Assessment tasks and items will range from 
understanding to creating level of the Revised Bloom‘s Taxonomy while the level of 
difficulty for the Final Examination items will range from understanding to evaluating 
level.  
The level of difficulty of the tasks or items will be determined by the following factors; 
(i) 
The real-life situation on a given Element of Construct that the learner will be 
expected to respond to. 
(ii) 
The subject knowledge required to respond to the real-life situation correctly.  
(iii) 
The complexity and/or number of cognitive processes required to generate responses 
to the items. 
(iv) 
The number of items a learner will be expected to respond to on a given real life 
situation. 
(v) 
The level of abstract thinking involved in responding to the task or item. 
(vi) 
The extent to which a learner or candidate has to devise a strategy to responding to 
the item as contrasted with items where the strategy is more explicit 
4. 
Scoring Criteria  
School Based Assessment tasks will be prepared and scored by the teachers in the schools. 
Allocation of marks for both School Based Assessment tasks and items other than Presentations and the 
Final Examination items will be based on the following guide:  
4.1 
Items or tasks involving calculations will be allocated method marks. The number of method marks 
will be determined by the appropriate stages involved in the solution to the task or item. 
4.2 
The correct answer in a calculation will be allocated an accuracy mark. 
4.3 
Marks will be allocated for correct diagrams (graphs) drawn and accuracy in measurements in the 
diagrams (graphs). 
4.4 
For tasks involving projects and practical activities, allocation of marks will be according to the 
Scoring Guide or Scoring Rubrics provided in the SBA Guidelines prepared by the ECZ. 
4.5 
Omission of essential working will result in loss of marks. Teachers should encourage learners to 
show their working where necessary.

Chapter 13: Agricultural Science – 4018  
1. 
Construct 
Applies scientific skills for sustainable food production and entrepreneurship. 
2. 
Elements of Construct  
The Agricultural Science examination will be based on the following six (6) Elements of Construct (EoC):  
EoC 1 
Investigates agricultural scientific problem through observations, measurement, 
experimentation and recording. 
 Identifies and defines agricultural problems and plans appropriate investigations. 
 Provides recommendations and solutions to solve real life agricultural problems. 
EoC 2 
Demonstrates practical skills in managing crops for improved yields. 
 Applies practical skills in crop management to solve real life problems and improve 
productivity. 
EoC 3 
Applies practical skills in managing livestock for improved production. 
 Applies practical skills in Livestock management to solve real life problems and improve 
productivity. 
EoC 4  Analyses data and applies sustainable scientific techniques for improved productivity. 
 Analyses and interprets agricultural data to draw valid scientific conclusions, 
 Applies sustainable agricultural techniques and uses findings to make informed decisions that 
improve productivity. 
EoC5 
Applies agribusiness and entrepreneurship skills in managing farm enterprises. 
 Develops and manages profitable agricultural enterprises through business planning, financial 
management, marketing, risk management, value addition and proper record keeping. 
 Demonstrate strong customer relations, communication, innovation and problem solving 
skills to improve enterprise performance. 
EoC 6 
 Investigates agricultural problems and demonstrates practical skills in real life situations. 
 Identifies agricultural problems and applies practical skills to solve them. 
3. 
Test Design

Assessment (SBA) and Component 2 will be the Final Examination. Component 1 will be set and 
administered by the school in Form 2 and 3 and Component 2 will be centrally set by the Examinations 
Council of Zambia (ECZ) at the end of Form 4. The weighting of School Based Assessment and Final 
Examination will be in a proportion of 30 percent to 70 percent respectively.

Structure

The School Based Assessment will consist of 12 tasks that should be completed across Form 2 and 
3. These tasks include a comparative research, a non-comparative field project, laboratory 
practicals, assignments and theory tests. All SBA tasks will be set and administered at school level.

include a comparative research to be conducted at form 3 level and a field project to be conducted at 
form 2 level; each with a total mark of 20. The School Based Assessment tasks will assess all the 
six (6) Elements of Construct. There will be six (6) tasks to be assessed at each level, that is Form 2 
and 3.

3.2 
Test Content 
The test content for the Agricultural Science assessments is specified per assessment component and 
type of task considering syllabus coverage and level of difficulty of the tasks. 
3.2.1. 
Types of Tasks and Item   
(i) School Based Assessment - Component 1 
The School Based Assessment will be based on all the Elements of Construct. Tasks 
under this component will be in form of class tests, end of term tests, end of year tests, 
projects, laboratory practicals, research and assignments.  These tasks will be based on 
all the six (6) Elements of Construct. 
The tasks assessing experimental skills and investigations may be based on materials 
and specimen which may not be familiar to the learners. In responding to such tasks, 
learners will be required to follow instructions, make observations, record data, draw 
diagrams, make inferences or conclusions and apply concepts and principles as 
outlined in the syllabus.

Learners will choose their projects and select topics for investigation from any topic 
laid down in the Agricultural Science Syllabus Secondary Education Ordinary Level 
Form 1 – 4 (CDC 2024).  
The projects may involve the investigation of growth, management, innovations and 
marketing of either a particular animal(s) or crop(s), machinery (s), or climate smart 
agriculture as suggested in the syllabus. The assignments, laboratory tests and theory 
tests will be conducted from all the Specific Competences across the syllabus.  
(ii) Final Examination - Component 2 
The Final Examination will be based on Elements of Construct 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5. This 
component will consist of semi-structured and essay type items which will require 
candidates to supply a written analysis or explanation to solve identified real life 
problems in agriculture. 
3.2.2 
Syllabus Coverage 
The Agricultural Science examination will be based on the subject content in the Form 1–4 
Agricultural Science Syllabus Secondary Education Ordinary Level (CDC 2024) and the 
key competences outlined in the Curriculum. Both assessment Components will ensure full 
coverage of the syllabus by incorporating a cross representation of concepts, themes, and 
sub-topics, while also assessing the Specific and Key Competences that learners are 
expected to develop. The assessment of key competences is important as this will: 
(i) help in aligning the curriculum with real life experiences and needs of learners, 
ensuring that education is relevant and practical; 
(ii) support the development of life long learning dispositions which are essential for 
preparing learners for future challenges and opportunities. 
In Component 2, a Test Specification has been developed to show the number of items that 
will be required to be set on each Element of Construct.  
Some items may introduce information that is unfamiliar to the candidates. In responding to 
such tasks, learners are required to use the competences developed through the syllabus 
such as reasoning, analysing, interpreting evidence and applying biological concepts to 
solve problems in new or unfamiliar situations. This aligns fully with the competence based 
approach, which emphasizes the use of knowledge and skills in practical real life contexts. 
3.2.3 
Level of Difficulty 
The level of difficulty of tasks in the School Based Assessments and test items for the Final 
Examination will be governed by the following factors:  
(i) The subject knowledge and skills required to respond to the real life problem and 
challenge correctly (level of competence); 
(ii) Complexity and/or number of cognitive processes required to generate responses to 
tasks and test items; 
(iii) The level of abstract and critical thinking involved; 
(iv) Extent to which the candidates have to strategise to respond to the tasks; 
(v) The complexity of the real life situation on a given Element of Construct a candidate 
will be expected to respond to correctly; 
(iv)  the number of tasks a learner will be expected to respond to on a given real life 
situation. 
Some tasks and items maybe based on unfamiliar materials. The expectation is that 
candidates should be able to apply scientific principles to generate responses to these types 
of tasks. To be successful at these tasks, candidates will need to draw on higher order

4. 
Scoring Criteria 
The following scoring criteria will govern the evaluation of the Examination for Certificate of Secondary 
Education Ordinary Level Agricultural Science assessments for both School Based Assessment and Final 
Examination. 
4.1 
School Based Assessment 
Teachers will develop and use appropriate scoring guides and rubrics for each SBA task. These 
scoring guides will ensure consistency, fairness and accuracy when evaluating learners‘ 
performance in activities such as class tests, projects, practical work, field investigations, 
presentations, assignments, and other structured tasks.  
These scoring guides or rubrics will guide teachers in evaluating learners‘ performance in practical 
work, research, projects, assignments, presentations, and other structured activities to ensure 
consistency, fairness and accuracy when evaluating learner performance. The scoring guides will 
measure not only the correctness of responses but also the application of scientific skills such as 
observation, data interpretation, problem solving, communication, and use of scientific procedures. 
This approach ensures accuracy, consistency and full alignment with the competence based learning 
activities in the Agricultural Science syllabus. 
Final Examination 
The Final Examination will consist of specific scoring guides and rubrics. The scoring guides will 
be developed by ECZ to support examiners in assessing candidates‘ responses based on the required 
competences and Elements of Construct. These scoring guides will outline the key scientific skills, 
reasoning processes and expected knowledge that demonstrate learner competence.  
Before marking begins, examiners will participate in a coordination meeting where acceptable 
alternative answers will be discussed to ensure that all valid responses are recognised. This process 
promotes consistency, fairness and accuracy in scoring.  
4.3 
General Marking Guidelines 
The following criteria govern the principles that are applied to marking the form 1-4 Agricultural 
Science assessments: 
(i) 
for essay questions in the Final Examination, the marks available per test item will be shown 
in brackets [ ] at the end of the item. 
(ii) 
the number of marks available per item will depend on the difficulty of the item i.e. the 
number of cognitive processes and the amount of writing required to generate a correct 
response.; 
(iii) where the response needs units, candidates are expected to supply the correct unit. Failure to 
do so will result in no mark being awarded even if the numerical value is correct; 
(iv) where working is required to be shown, and a candidate supplies a correct numerical response 
with wrong working or no working shown, no marks will be awarded
