Chapter 25: Computer Science – 8010 
1. 
Construct 
Demonstrates proficiency in designing, developing, and applying computational solutions to real-world 
problems, guided by Computer Science principles and prioritising ethics and responsible digital 
citizenship. 
2. 
Elements of Construct 
The Computer Science Examination for Certificate of Secondary Education Ordinary Level will be based 
on five (5) Elements of Construct (EoC) as follows: 
EoC1 Recognises computer system components to build and troubleshoot computer systems 
everyday life 
• 
Identifies computer components, understands their functions, and uses this knowledge to 
assemble and troubleshoot computer systems in everyday situations. 
EoC2  Analyses, computer tools based on their roles in problem solving 
• 
Analyses, classifies, and distinguishes different computer tools based on how each one is 
used to solve specific problems. 
EoC3  Analyses computing concepts by identifying relationships, principles and underlying 
structures 
• 
Investigates core computing concepts and understanding how they interconnect within digital 
systems. 
EoC4 Applies computational knowledge to critically evaluate situations and make informed 
technological decisions 
• 
Applies computational thinking to analyse problems, evaluate technological     options, and 
make responsible, well-reasoned decisions based on efficiency, ethics, safety, and long-term 
impact. 
EoC5 Creates and designs systems that solve real life problems and improve way of life for citizens  
• 
Designs, develops, and produces digital solutions that are creative, functional, user-centred, 
and address real-world societal needs. 
3.

Assessment (SBA) which will be set and scored by teachers at school level in Form 2 and 3. Component 2

the end of Form 4.

3.1 
Structure 
The School Based Assessment will consist of sixteen (16) tasks designed to assess learners‘ 
practical understanding and application of Computer Science competences. These tasks will be 
based on the Computer Science Construct and will include class exercises, practical tests, 
assignments, and a project completed either individually or in groups. 
(i) 
Problem Solving Exercises: These will involve problem solving tasks that require critical 
computational thinking and problem-solving skills. These will be done through quizzes, 
assignments and class collaboration tasks. 
(ii) 
Practical Tests:  Learners will take hands-on tasks such as, handling files, creating 
algorithms, code writing, programs to solve real world problems. 
(iii) Coding Assignment: Learners will apply problem solving skills and logic, write simple 
programs, debug and test solutions.  It will involve real-world problems. 
(iv) Portfolio Assessment: Learners will be required to maintain a portfolio showcasing their 
coding projects, solutions, and reflections on their learning journey. This portfolio can be 
stored offline on a hard drive or flash drive, or online using cloud services such as Google 
Drive or Dropbox. 
(v)     Peer Review: Learners will provide constructive feedback on each other‘s work to encourage 
collaboration and teamwork. 
(vi) Project: A multi-term project requiring: 
 problem definition 
 algorithm design 
 coding / prototype creation 
 testing 
 documentation 
Through these activities, learners will develop the competences outlined in the Computer Science 
syllabus, while enhancing their analytical thinking, computational skills, creativity, digital problem-
solving abilities, and real-life application of Computer Science knowledge. The tasks will enable 
learners to design solutions, interpret and analyse data, apply Computer Science tools effectively, 
and address authentic problems using appropriate digital technologies.

The School-Based Assessment tasks at Form 2 and Form 3 will be drawn from topics relevant 
to each form. These tasks will be based on the competences expected at each level, as 
outlined in the assessment framework for Computer Science. 
Types of Tasks 
The SBA will comprise real-life scenarios and practical tasks requiring learners to: 
(i) 
Design and implement algorithms and programs. 
(ii) 
Develop and analyse flowcharts and pseudocode. 
(iii) 
Collect, organise and interpret data. 
(iv) 
Apply problem-solving strategies to practical computing challenges. 
(v) 
Evaluate and propose solutions using appropriate digital tools and technologies. 
(vi) 
Create simple digital projects that demonstrate creativity and technical skills. 
3.2.2 Final Examination  Component 2 
The Final Examination will assess learners‘ ability to apply and demonstrate competences in 
Computer Science through structured and scenario-based tasks from the five (5) Elements of 
Construct. 
Types of Items 
The examination will include items drawn from real-life computing situations, requiring 
learners to: 
(i) 
Analyse problems and propose suitable algorithmic or digital solutions. 
(ii) 
Apply programming and computational thinking skills. 
(iii) 
Interpret data, system diagrams, and program outputs. 
(iv) 
Evaluate digital solutions and justify decision-making processes. 
(v) 
Create simple programs, flowcharts, or system designs to address given problems. 
3.3 
Level of Difficulty 
For the School-Based Assessment (SBA) tasks, the level of difficulty will range from Remembering 
to Creating, in accordance with revised Bloom‘s Taxonomy. For the Final Examination, item 
difficulty will range from Understanding to Analysing. 
The difficulty level of each SBA task or examination question will be guided by the following 
factors: 
(i) 
The real-life Computer Science situation presented, requiring an appropriate learner response. 
(ii) 
The depth of subject knowledge needed to address the scenario or task accurately. 
(iii) 
The complexity and number of cognitive processes involved in generating correct responses. 
(iv) 
The number of questions or subtasks a learner must complete within a given scenario. 
(v) 
The level of abstract thinking and technical reasoning required to solve the problem. 
(vi) 
The degree of strategy, independence, and decision-making expected, especially when 
learners must devise their own approach rather than follow explicitly provided steps. 
These factors ensure that both SBA and Final Examination tasks effectively develop and assess 
learners‘ problem-solving, critical thinking, creativity, digital literacy, and practical Computer 
Science competences.
