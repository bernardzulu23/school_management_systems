3. 
Test Design 
3.1  
Structure  
The Physics examination will be made up of two components namely Component 1 and 2. The 
School Based Assessment will be component 1 while the final examination will be component 2. 
3.1.1 School Based Assessment: Component 1 
The assessments in SBA will be administered during the course of teaching and learning at 
school level from Form 2 Term 1 to Form 3 Term 3.  It shall account for 30% of the total 
assessment. The structure of SBA tasks will be determined by the type of task and by the 
teacher.  
3.1.2 Final Examination: Component 2 
The Final Examination will be written at the end of form 4 and will be centrally set by the 
Examinations Council of Zambia. Component 2 shall be a theory paper consisting of six (6) 
compulsory items of varying marks. The component will have a total of 80 marks. The 
duration for the component will be 2 hours 30 minutes. It shall account for 70% of the total 
assessment.

3.2 
Test Content  
 
The test content for both components 1 and 2 will be considered in terms of test items, level 
of difficulty and syllabus coverage. 
3.2.1 
School Based Assessment: Component 1 
The SBA tasks will be based on all the six Elements of Construct. These tasks shall be in 
form of theory, practical work and research/project done during the teaching and learning 
process in the school as formative and summative assessments. 
Types of Tasks 
Component 1 will comprise tests, assignments, practical and research/project work among 
other tasks. All the tasks will consist of a scenario depicting a real-life situations containing 
a problem or challenge. The SBA items and tasks will be based on all the six Elements of 
Construct and will comprise semi-structured, essay or open ended items, investigative or 
practical work and projects/research work. 
3.2.2 
Final Examination Component 2 
This will consist of semi-structured and open ended type of items. There shall be six (6) 
compulsory items based on scenarios depicting real life problems. 
Semi-structured  items: These items shall include a scenario presented as a real-life 
situation, in form of a diagram, graph, table, picture, or a short stem, followed by a number 
of sub-items. Candidates shall be required to provide answers using words, numbers, 
symbols, phrases, definitions, comparisons and calculations.  
Open ended: These items will require candidates to answer in complete sentences and 
paragraphs, presenting their responses in a logical and coherent manner in order to respond 
to the competence being sought. 
3.2.3 
Level of Difficulty 
The level of difficulty of tasks/items in the physics assessments for School Based 
Assessment will range from remembering to creating level of the Revised Bloom‘s 
Taxonomy while the level of difficulty for the Final Examination items will range from 
remembering to Evaluating level. The level of difficulty will be determined by the 
following factors:  
(i) 
the real-life situation on a given Element of Construct that the learner will be 
exposed to; 
(ii) 
the amount and type of subject knowledge required to respond to the real-life 
situation correctly; 
(iii) 
the complexity and/or number of cognitive processes required to generate responses 
to tasks or items; 
(iv) 
the level of abstract thinking involved; 
(v) 
the level of scientific principles required to generate responses correctly;

3.2.4 
Syllabus Coverage 
The Physics assessments will be based on the competences in the Form 1–4 Physics 
syllabus (CDC 2024). Both assessment components will ensure full coverage of the 
Elements of Construct by incorporating a cross-representation of the key competences in 
Physics. 
In Component  2,  a Test framework has been developed to show the number of items that 
will be required to be set on each Element of Construct.  
Some items may introduce information that is unfamiliar to the candidates. In responding 
to such tasks, learners are required to use the competences developed through the syllabus 
such as reasoning, analysing, interpreting evidence, and applying Physics concepts to solve 
problems in new or unfamiliar situations. This aligns fully with the competency-based 
approach, which emphasises on the use of acquired competences in solving practical, real-
life challenges. 
4. 
Scoring Criteria 
The following scoring criteria will govern the evaluation of the Examination for Certificate of  
Secondary Education Ordinary Level and General Certificate of Education Physics assessments for both: 
School Based Assessment (SBA) - Component 1 and Final Examination - Component 2. 
4.1 
School-Based Assessment (SBA) - Component 1 
Teachers will develop and use appropriate scoring tools such as scoring guides and scoring rubrics 
for each task e.g. end of year tests. These scoring tools will ensure consistency, fairness, and 
accuracy when evaluating learners‘ performance in activities such as end of term and year tests, 
practical work, assignments, and other structured items. The scoring criteria will reflect the 
expected Physics competences as outlined in the syllabus. 
The scoring criteria will measure not only the correctness of responses but also the application of 
scientific skills such as observation, data interpretation, problem solving, communication, and use 
of scientific procedures. This approach ensures accuracy, consistency, and full alignment with the 
competency-based learning activities in the Physics syllabus. 
4.2  
Final Examination - Component 2 
The Final Examination will consist of specific scoring procedures to maintain acceptable standards. 
The scoring criteria will be provided by the Examinations Council of Zambia. 
For component 2, scoring criteria will be developed by the Examinations Council of Zambia to 
support examiners/scorers in assessing candidates. These scoring guides will outline the key 
scientific skills, reasoning processes, expected knowledge that demonstrate a learner‘s competences.

This section highlights important notes and subject-specific guidelines that candidates and teachers must 
follow during the administration and scoring of the Physics assessments. These guidelines ensure fairness, 
consistency, and alignment with the competence-based Physics syllabus. 
5.1 
General Examination Guidelines 
(i) 
Candidates must sit for both components to be graded. 
(ii) 
All responses for Component 2 must be written in the spaces provided within the question 
paper. 
(iii) 
Programmable Calculators, cell phones, and unauthorised materials are not allowed in the 
examination room. 
(iv) 
Non-programmable calculators are allowed in the examination room. 
5.2 
Physics-Specific Guidelines 
(i) 
Diagrams must be large, neat, correctly labelled, and drawn with a sharp pencil. Shading and 
colouring are not allowed unless instructed. 
(ii) 
When answering data-based questions, candidates must show working steps, units, and 
explanations where required. 
(iii) 
For tasks involving experiments or investigations, candidates must follow the scientific 
method, showing observations, reasoning, and conclusions. 
(iv) 
Scientific terminology must be used accurately. Vague, non-scientific statements will not 
earn full marks. 
(v) 
In items involving unfamiliar scenarios or materials, candidates must apply principles and 
concepts from the syllabus to make logical conclusions. 
(vi) 
Structured-type of items must be written in clear, coherent paragraphs with ideas presented 
logically to reflect higher-order competences.

Item 
The effects of waves on humans, their habitats and the environment in general, depend not only on types but also on their 
source, intensity/frequency and the duration of exposure to them. A form 4 class spent one of their holidays with an assignment 
to analyse effects of some waves. Wave type A may cause landslides for people that live near river banks among other effects. 
Wave type B caused internal heating of body tissues potentially leading to damage to organs and development of cataracts after 
excessive exposure despite being useful in warming foods in homes. Wave type C, despite providing an essential vitamin to 
people, caused skin cancer and eye damage with excessive exposure. 
Now do the following tasks: 
(a) 
(i) 
Describe the wave type A by providing its identity and how it is generated. Discuss other probable effects of this 
type of waves other than the one stated in the problem statement.  
 [3 marks] 
(ii) 
The waves identified in part (a) (i) are suspected to have been generated 1 000km from the river banks and have a 
frequency of 0.01Hz with a wavelength of 5m. Determine how long the waves will take to reach the river banks. 
 
 [2 marks] 
Answer 
− 
Wave type A is a ground movement triggered by various forces, an example is seismic wave. A seismic wave is an earth 
generated kind of wave which can destabilise slope and cause them to fall. It is classified in three types:

For wave type C, two eye defects were noted: some group of people were unable to see distant objects while others were 
unable to see nearby objects. Identify and discuss the two defects and use the idea of lenses to suggest how the defects 
can be overcome.  
 [3 marks] 
Answer 
− 
People who were unable to see distant objects are said to be near sightedness or to have myopia condition. Those 
who were unable to see nearby objects are said to be hypermetropia condition or far-sightedness. 
In myopia, the person can see the objects nearby but cannot see distant objects clearly as they appear blurry and 
the person will not be comfortable seeing them. This condition takes place when the shape of the eyes leads the 
light rays to bend in a wrong way, focusing images in front of the retina rather than focusing light on the retina. 
In hypermetropic, the person can see objects at a distance but cannot see or squints to see nearby objects. This 
condition is caused when the light rays from a close by object are focused at a point behind the retina. 
How these defects are corrected using lenses: - 
− 
Myopia; use spectacles with concave or minus lenses of suitable power to help focus light (or image) 
correctly on the retina. 
− 
Use contact lenses to provide a wider field of vision. 
− 
Hypermetropic; use spectacles with a convex lens to impart additional focus power to help form the image 
on the retina. 
Another condition for people above the age of 40, having difficulties in seeing nearby objects is 
presbyopia. This happens when the ciliary muscle weakens and diminish the elasticity of the eye lens. This 
condition is corrected by use of appropriate eye glasses or contact lenses or minor surgery to restore vision 
clarity.
