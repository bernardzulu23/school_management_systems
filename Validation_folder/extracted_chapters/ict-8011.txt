Chapter 26: Information and Communications Technology  - 8011  
 
1 
Construct 
Applies digital literacy and computational understanding to interpret problems, create solution-driven 
approaches, and use Information and Communications Technology (ICT) responsibly and effectively in 
real-life applications. 
2 
Elements of Construct 
The Information and Communications Technology (ICT) Examination candidates will be assessed using 
the following five (5) Elements of Construct (EoC):  
EoC1 
Demonstrate the ability to identify and describe ICT components, systems and their 
functions 
 Recognises computer components and explain how input, processing, storage, and output 
devices work together to perform tasks.  
EoC2 
Classify and distinguish ICT tools, file types, network structures, and technologies 
 Categorises ICT tools, differentiates file formats, and compares network topologies and 
technologies 
EoC3 
Develops analytical skills and evaluating ICT systems and problems 
 Assesses system behaviour processes, resolve operational issues, and recommend 
configuration and reliable improvements 
EoC4 
Evaluate ICT systems, networks and technologies 
 Examines ICT systems and technologies by assessing operational performance, comparing 
alternatives, and considering sustainability, security, and user impact. 
EoC5 
Applies ethical reasoning in the responsible use of ICT 
 Evaluates a candidate‘s knowledge of digital rights, privacy, and security, and their ability to 
use technology responsibly and safely. 
3

summary of the test design for School Based Assessment and Final Examination.

   3.1 
Structure 
The ICT assessment will comprise two components: School Based Assessment (SBA) – 
Component 1 and the Final Examination – Component 2. Each component evaluates specific 
competences and contributes differently to the candidates‘ overall performance. 
3.1.1 
School Based Assessment (SBA) – Component 1 
The SBA will include both formative and summative assessment tasks administered by the 
teacher at school level during teaching and learning sessions from Form 2 to Form 3. It will 
involve a combination of practical and theoretical activities. 
The SBA is divided into two parts: Part 1 and Part 2. 
(a) Part 1: Continuous Assessment Tasks 
Part 1 will assess learners through a variety of practical and theoretical tasks, including: 
(i) 
Project assessments 
(ii) 
Class participation and collaboration tasks 
(iii) 
Peer review and group work 
(iv) 
Software Simulation tasks 
(v) 
Paper-based practical activities 
(vi) 
Oral examinations

(vii) Problem-solving exercises 
(viii) Portfolio assessment 
These tasks will address all Elements of Competence (EoC1 to EoC5) and will consist 
of fifteen (15) tasks, amounting to a total of 200 marks. This section will be completed 
over two years, allowing for continuous evaluation as learners progressively build their 
ICT skills. 
(b) Part 2: Final Project Presentation 
Part 2 of the SBA will consist of one major taskthe Final Project Presentation. 
Learners will be assessed on: 
(i) 
Their ability to demonstrate applied ICT skills 
(ii) 
The clarity and rationale behind their project choices 
(iii) 
Their ability to respond effectively to questions 
The final project presentation will cover all five Elements of Construct (Eo1–Eo5) and 
will carry 100 marks. This project will be undertaken over two years, from Form 2 
Term 1 to Form 3 Term 1, enabling learners to integrate and apply knowledge 
independently. 
3.1.2 
Final Examination – Component 2 
Component 2 will include structured open-ended items, essays, and scenario-based items 
which will be based on the ICT Construct. 
The paper will consist of two sections: Section A and Section B as follows: 
(a) Section A (Compulsory) 
Section A will be compulsory and will be divided into Part A and Part B: 
Part A: 
This part will contain three (03) items based on EoC1, EoC2, and EoC3 using key 
competences such as Digital Literacy, Environmental Sustainability, Communication 
and Entrepreneurship. 
The items will be structured and open-ended scenario-based, carrying a total of 50 
marks. 
Part B: 
This part will contain one (01) item based on EoC4 from Competences such as Critical 
Thinking, Financial Education and Problem Solving. 
The item will include scenario-based and essay-type tasks and will carry 30 marks.  
All items in Section A will be compulsory, contributing a total of 80 marks. 
(b) Section B (Optional) 
Section B will consist of three (03) optional items based on EoC5 from competences 
such Emotional Intelligence and Citizenship 
Candidates will be required to answer two (02) items only by choosing from the three 
items. Each will carry 20 marks. 
3.2 
Test Content 
 The SBA is designed to assess a learner‘s practical skills, knowledge, and application of concepts 
over a period of time. 
3.2.1 School-Based Assessment (SBA) – Component 1 
The School-Based Assessment at Form 2, and Form 3, 4 will consist of tasks aligned to the 
competences required at each level, as outlined in the Information and Communications

Technology Assessment framework. SBA will assess both theoretical understanding and 
practical ICT skills, using a variety of continuous assessment activities. These tasks should be 
administered in all schools except those with limited facilities where an Alternative-to-
Practical approach will be used. 
 (a) 
Types of SBA Tasks 
SBA in ICT will draw from a diverse range of activities designed to develop and 
measure learners‘ practical competence, problem-solving ability, and theoretical 
knowledge. 
(i) Project Assessments: Evaluate learner‘s ability to apply ICT concepts and 
practical skills in completing real or simulated tasks. 
(ii) Problem-Solving Exercises: Assess learners‘ critical thinking, analytical skills, 
and ability to propose logical solutions to ICT-related problems. 
(iii) Class Participation and Collaboration: Measure learner engagement, 
teamwork, communication, and contribution during practical and theoretical 
lessons. 
(iv) Portfolio Assessment: Involves reviewing a curated collection of student work 
demonstrating progress in skills development and understanding over time. 
(v) Final Project Presentation: Provides an opportunity for learners to present 
and justify their project work, demonstrating clarity, confidence, and 
comprehension. 
(b) 
Alternative-to-Practical Examinations 
In schools with limited access to functional computer labs, learners will be assessed 
using equivalent tasks that measure the same competences expected in practical 
settings. These include: 
(i) Simulation Software: Enables learners to practise data management, networks, 
and system operations in a virtual environment. 
(ii) Paper-Based Practical Tasks: Require students to outline procedural steps, 
design network diagrams, create flowcharts, and plan data-handling strategies. 
(iii) Project-Based Assessments: Focus on designing theoretical ICT solutions that 
demonstrate understanding of concepts and processes. 
(iv) Oral Examinations: Allow students to verbally explain procedures for 
practical tasks, such as troubleshooting, and network security processes. 
(v) Peer Review and Group Work: Encourage collaborative learning where 
learners present tasks to peers for structured feedback. 
(vi) Shift-Based Use of ICT Labs: Rotational schedules ensure equitable access to 
computers where resources are limited. 
(vii) Portfolio Assessments: Include written assignments, design plans, reports, and 
reflections compiled throughout the learning period. 
3.2.2 Final Examination – Component 2 
The Final Examination evaluates candidates‘ ability to apply ICT knowledge and demonstrate 
key competences through structured questions, case studies, scenario-based tasks, and essay-
style responses.

             The final Examination will comprise two sections, A and B 
(i) Section A – Compulsory 
All candidates must attempt this section. It is divided into Part A and Part B: 
Part A: Contains three items, each structured, open-ended, and based on real 
or contextual scenarios. Candidates must interpret the situations and provide

Part B: Contains one (1) item that combines scenario-based questioning with

(ii) Section B – Optional

tasks, either scenario-based or in case-study format. Candidates will select only 
two items to answer. 
3.4 
Level of Difficulty 
SBA task difficulty will span from lower-order skills (Remembering) to higher-order skills 
(Creating) in line with Bloom‘s Taxonomy. 
For the Final Examination, the difficulty range will focus on Understanding through Analysing. 
The level of difficulty for both SBA and examination items will be determined by: 
(i)   
The nature of the real-life ICT situation presented and the type of response required. 
(ii) 
The depth of content knowledge necessary to respond accurately. 
(iii) 
The complexity and number of cognitive processes needed to complete the task. 
(iv)    The number and structure of subtasks embedded within the scenario. 
(v) 
The degree of abstract reasoning and technical interpretation involved. 
(vi)  The level of independence, decision-making, and strategic thinking expected, especially when 
learners must develop their own solutions rather than follow a prescribed process. 
These considerations will ensure that all tasks both SBA and the Final Examination—appropriately 
measure and promote learners‘ specific competences in ICT, including critical thinking, problem-
solving, creativity, digital skills, and practical understanding. 
3.5 
Syllabus Coverage 
The content for School-Based Assessment (SBA) will be selected from the ICT syllabus for Form 2 
through Form 4 in accordance with the competence requirements outlined for each grade. SBA will 
emphasise practical engagement and the development of key abilities such as analytical thinking, 
creativity, digital competence, and effective problem-solving. 
The Final Examination will be based on content from the material from the entire Information and 
Communications Technology Secondary Education Ordinary Level Form 1–Form 4 Syllabus, to 
ensure that learners are assessed on a full spectrum of knowledge and skills, from basic principles to 
applied and analytical ICT competences. 
4 
Scoring Criteria 
4.1 
School Based Assessment 
SBA tasks will be set, assessed and scored internally by the subject teacher and moderated 
periodically throughout the course. This ensures learners‘ performance is measured accurately and 
consistently over time.

4.2 
Final Examination 
The Final Examination will be externally prepared and centrally evaluated. 
4.2.1 
All candidate scripts will be collected for central marking by assigned examiners.  
4.2.2 
The Examinations Council of Zambia will provide an official marking scheme to ensure 
uniformity and fairness in grading. 
4.2.3 
Acceptable alternative responses will be included in the marking scheme and finalised 
during an examiners‘ coordination meeting. 
5 
Notes and Guidelines 
The following guidelines apply to all candidates and centres undertaking the ICT examination: 
5.1 The weighting for Component 1 will be 30% while component 2 , the Final Examination will    
        be 70%.   
5.2 
Candidates are required to complete both the SBA and Final Examinations components to be graded. 
5.3  
Eligibility is limited to candidates registered with the Examinations Council of Zambia (ECZ) via 
their respective centres. 
5.4 
Examination rules and instructions will be provided on the cover page of the question paper and in 
the annual examination timetable. 
5.5 
All projects, practical tasks, and other submissions must comply with the prescribed format and 
submission requirements. 
5.6 
Guidelines for GCE candidates will be developed by ECZ. 
5.7  
Candidates shall NOT be allowed to enter the examination room with a phone or calculator of any 
kind or Personal Digital Assistant (PDA).
